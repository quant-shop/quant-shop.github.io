Discriminatory Laws in the U.S.

1. Slavery and Black Codes

Slave Codes (Colonial–1865)

  • Sample Language (Virginia Slave Code, 1705):
    “All Negro, mulatto and Indian slaves within this dominion… shall be held to be real estate.”

Black Codes (1865–1866)

  • Mississippi Black Code, Nov. 1865
    “No negro or freedman shall be permitted to rent or lease any lands or tenements except in incorporated cities or towns…”

2. Jim Crow Era State Statutes (1870s–1965)

  • Alabama Constitution of 1901, Art. IV, Sec. 102
    “The legislature shall never pass any law to authorize or legalize any marriage between any white person and a negro, or descendant of a negro.”

  • Florida, Laws of 1885, Ch. 3166, Sec. 1
    “All marriages between a white person and a negro, or between a white person and a person of negro descent to the fourth generation inclusive, are hereby forever prohibited.”

  • Mississippi Constitution, 1890, Art. VIII, Sec. 207
    “Separate schools shall be maintained for the children of the white and colored races.”

  • Texas Revised Civil Statutes, Art. 2900 (1909) “The board of trustees … shall have the power to establish and maintain separate schools for white and colored children.”

  • Oklahoma Statutes, 1915, Sec. 4353 “It shall be unlawful for a white person to marry any person of African descent.”

  • Georgia, Act of 1926 (Transportation Segregation) “All passenger stations in this state operated by any motor transportation company shall have separate waiting rooms or space and separate ticket windows for the white and colored races.”

3. Native American Land and Autonomy

  • Indian Removal Act, 1830 (Public Law 21-148, May 28, 1830) > Authorized the President “to cause so much of any territory belonging to the United States, west of the river Mississippi … to be divided into a suitable number of districts…” (forced removals, e.g., Trail of Tears).

  • Dawes Act, 1887 (24 Stat. 388, ch. 119) > “An Act to provide for the allotment of lands in severalty to Indians on the various reservations…” (breaking up collective landholding by Native Nations).

4. Immigration Exclusion

  • Chinese Exclusion Act, 1882, ch. 126, 22 Stat. 58 > “An Act to execute certain treaty stipulations relating to Chinese. …the coming of Chinese laborers to the United States be…suspended…”

  • Immigration Act of 1924 (Johnson–Reed Act), Pub.L. 68–139, 43 Stat. 153 > Established national origins quotas barring or strictly limiting entry from Asia, Africa, and southern/eastern Europe.

5. Voting Suppression

  • Louisiana Constitution of 1898, Art. 197 (Grandfather Clause) > “No male person who was on January 1st, 1867, or prior thereto, entitled to vote… and no descendant of such person, shall be denied the right to register and vote…”

  • Alabama Constitution of 1901, Art. VIII, Sec. 194 (Poll Tax) > “No person shall be eligible to vote at any election unless he shall have paid… all poll taxes due…”

6. Housing, Lending, and Economic Racial Segregation

  • Federal Housing Administration, Underwriting Manual, c.1938–1960s
    > “Incompatible racial groups should not be permitted to live in the same communities.”

  • Restrictive Covenants (enforced pre-1948; language in deeds/city ordinances) > “No property in said addition shall be used, owned or occupied by any person of the Negro or Mongolian race.”

7. Landmark Federal Anti-Discrimination Legislation

  • Civil Rights Act of 1964 (Pub.L. 88–352, 78 Stat. 241, July 2, 1964)
    • Title II (42 U.S.C. §2000a):
      “All persons shall be entitled to the full and equal enjoyment … of any place of public accommodation, as defined … without discrimination or segregation on the ground of race, color, religion, or national origin.”
    • Title VI (42 U.S.C. §2000d):
      “No person in the United States shall, on the ground of race, color, or national origin, be excluded from participation in… federally assisted program…”
  • Voting Rights Act of 1965 (Pub.L. 89–110, 79 Stat. 437, Aug. 6, 1965)
    • Section 2:
      “No voting qualification or prerequisite to voting, or standard, practice, or procedure … shall be imposed or applied by any State or political subdivision to deny or abridge the right of any citizen of the United States to vote on account of race or color…”
  • Fair Housing Act of 1968 (Title VIII of the Civil Rights Act of 1968, 42 U.S.C. §§3601-3619)
    • “It shall be unlawful to refuse to sell or rent … or otherwise make unavailable or deny, a dwelling to any person because of race, color, religion, or national origin.”

8. Supreme Court Decisions: Codifying and Striking Down Discrimination

  • Plessy v. Ferguson, 163 U.S. 537 (1896) > “Laws permitting, and even requiring, their separation in places where they are liable to be brought into contact, do not necessarily imply the inferiority of either race…”

  • Brown v. Board of Education, 347 U.S. 483 (1954) > “Separate educational facilities are inherently unequal.”

9. References

  • All statutes and court decisions are cited as per their official publication. For full text, refer to state legislative archives, U.S. Statutes at Large, or Supreme Court opinions.
